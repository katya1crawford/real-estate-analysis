Seller Disclosure Statement

Ask seller to complete Seller Disclosure Statement (Google "seller disclosure statements", your hits will include forms from multiple states. Or ask broker) that lists and explains all known problems or defects that may plague a property. If the seller refuses, offset this additional risk by scaling down the price you offer and enhance the rigor of the property inspection.

Read this document carefully, so you'll know immediately about any issues with the property that the owner knew about. Of course, the large loophole here is that the owner must know about the problems.

As you review a seller-completed disclosure statement, watch for these six issues:

  1. Sellers cannot disclose facts or conditions of which they are unaware. Disclosures do not substitute for inspections.
  2. Disclosures reveal the known past. They make no guarantees. Sellers do not warrant the present or future condition of the property. They report only what they know.
  3. Disclosure questions permit subjective answers. Are playing children a neighborhood noise problem? Is a planned street widening an adverse condition?
  4. Disclosure statements may not require sellers to disclose property defects that you can readily see. Inspection supersedes disclosure.
  5. Beware of seller (or agent) statements that begin, "I believe", "I think", "as far as we know", and other similar hedges. Do not accept these answers as the final word. Follow up hedged statements or assertions with definitive inquiry or inspection.
  6. Many non-occupant owners claim no knowledge as to defects and condition. Their disclosure form often lack substantive answers. But they will maintain a record of service calls, maintenance, and replacements - as these records are required by the IRS. Ask the seller to produce or adjust your offer accordingly.

Seller disclosures help you understand and value properties. But even so, give them only as much weight as they deserve. Some sellers forget that their boiler bangs like a tin drum; or maybe a circuit breaker pops each time a toaster and microwave operate at the same time. Sellers often fail to disclose.

Income Properties

Most states, if not all, require that the seller disclose any known defects in a property before selling it. Therefore, in most deals you purchase with the help of a real estate agent, the seller will give you a pile of forms in which the seller lists anything that might be wrong with the property. However, some seller disclosure laws apply only to one-to-four family owner-occupied properties. If you buy an apartment building or shopping center, the law may not require the seller to fill out a disclosure statement. If, in this situation, the seller refuses, offset this additional risk by scaling down the price you offer, and enhance the rigor of your pre-purchase inspections.
