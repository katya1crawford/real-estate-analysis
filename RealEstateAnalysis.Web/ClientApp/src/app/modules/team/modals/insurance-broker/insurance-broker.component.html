Commercial Property Insurance Broker

Look for an insurance broker, rather than just an agent at a specific company. A broker can shop around at different companies for the best rate and coverage, and can switch your policy if necessary without you having to take your business elsewhere.

Find commercial property insurance broker by getting referral from property management company, real estate agent, real estate investors, attorney, CPA. You want an agent who has experience in insuring the type and size of property you’re looking to acquire. Ask him about his background and experience.

Get few insurance quotes on the property while doing your due diligence.

You’ll need the agent when you get ready to close your deal on a property, as no lender is going to fund a loan on an uninsured building.

If you have multiple properties, see if you can have them covered under the same insurance policy. You might be able to get discount that way.

Make sure your tenants have renter's insurance and entity that owns property is listed as an additional interest on that policy.

If initial numbers on property look great, before you move forward on that property call insurance broker to see what will be the cost of insurance for that property. Also, insurance company will pull a lot of data about that property and the surrounding area. Ask insurance company if they can share that information with you. So you can take a look at that and find out what quality of that property is, what you can expect to receive from rental income, what your flood exposure is, your earthquake exposure is etc. See maybe there is flood issue, maybe there is losses in the past you weren't made aware of that can harm your ability to obtain cost-effective insurance.

Insurance for Landlords

The replacement cost policy is the preferred option if you can get it. The coverage will pay for the full cost of a covered loss minus your deductible.

When looking at a replacement cost policy, make sure the dwelling coverage (Coverage A) involves enough of a payment to rebuild, base on what contractors are charging in your area.

With most policies, if you are more than 20% under market costs, you will be subject to a coinsurance penalty. This means that if the home would cost $200,000 to rebuild, and you are insured for less than $160,000 (80% of the value), any claim you have will be paid out at the same ratio. For easy math, if the value is $200,000, and you insure for $100,000, you are at 50%. If you have a toilet overflow that causes $8,000 in damage, the insurance company would pay only 50% of the cost - $4000 minus the deductible. This is because you were only insured at 50% value on the home. Watch for this, and make sure your house is insured to full replacement value.

Next, make sure your policy includes outbuildings (garage, shops, etc.), if you have any. Also look for "loss of rents coverage". If your renters have to move out after a fire or other covered damage to the home, this will pay you the rent you would have collected from them during that time.

Lastly, make sure you have liability coverage on the property. Common limits are $300,000, $500,000 and $1,000,000. It doesn’t usually cost much to get the higher coverages, so it’s almost always worth it. Make sure you renters also carry their own renter’s insurance as well. Don’t let tenants have dangerous dogs or other pets. You don’t want want a dog bite claim on your record. Insurance companies have those.

Before you buy a fire insurance policy and think you’re covered, talk with several insurance agents and ask these questions:

  • Who is covered? Typically, your insurance coverage protects only the named insureds within the policy. Therefore, take care to name all persons or companies (LLC owning property, co-owners) whose financial interest in the property deserves protection. Make sure that LLC owning property is first named insured. Add Wyoming holding LLC as a named insured also. You may also list your name under named insured to satisfy your lender's requirements and accept that insurance. Put your lender as additional insured. If you have multiple property holding LLCs have them all listed under named insured if you have them all under the same insurance policy.
  • What property is covered? On this point, insurance policies distinguish between the property you own and the property your tenants own. Policies seldom (if ever) cover tenants’ property (require them to buy their own tenant policy). Also, policies distinguish between personal property (appliances, lawn care equipment, assorted tools, and other items stored in the garage) and real property (the building itself, plants, shrubs, fencing).
  • What perils are covered? People think that fire represents the most critical peril to protect against. But what about flood, earthquake, vandalism, tenant abuse, windstorm, hail, mudslides, sinkholes, riot, theft, accumulation of snow and ice (on roofs and collapse), termite infestation, lawsuits, trip-and-fall accident, and other potential losses too numerous te mention? Your policy will list covered perils. Read them. If not adequate, your insurer may add coverage throughout a rider or endorsement.
  • What losses are covered? Insurers usually classify losses as direct or indirect. A fire that damages your building creates a direct loss. The fact that you can no longer collect rents until the damage is repaired represents an indirect loss. Your basic policy will not cover many types of indirect losses. For these losses (if coverage is available), you need to buy a rider or endorsement.
  • How much will the insurer pay? Every insurance contract restricts how much it will pay for losses. Most policies include some combination of deductibles, limits, and co-insurance. Also, for real or personal property, your policy might pay to replace, repair, or rebuild the damaged items with new materials (100 percent of replacement cost). Or the policy might only reimburse actual cash value (replacement cost new, less depreciation).

Within the same policy, different types and amounts of restrictions apply to the various types of coverage. For example, fire damage might subject you to a $500 deductible, whereas earthquake damage subjects you to a $10,000 deductible. Don’t assume that your policy provides the amounts of coverage you want or need. Ask your insurance agent to explain all of the ways the company may restrict or limit its payments for your covered losses.

Even when you buy replacement coverage for your building, your insurer may not pay for repair or construction costs mandated by stricter building codes. Without a code compliance endorsement, your policy will pay only to replace your current building as it existed before it suffered damage. If, to rebuild, current code requires additional fire sprinklers or an upgraded 200-amp electrical system, you’ll pay these costs out of pocket. Example from book, a large oak tree recently fell onto one of my houses. Code compliance coverage added around $25,000 to my insurance recovery - an amount that otherwise would have come out of my pocket.

Improve Coverage & Reduce Premiums

After choosing your basic coverages and endorsements, search through various policy deductibles and limits. Fine tune to identify the most cost-effective combinations. Avoid paying a lot for a little: save money; increase deductibles and eliminate trivial coverages that disproportionately push up your premiums. Also avoid risking a lot for a little. Purchase coverages that protect you against large-scale, budget-busting losses such as huge liability claims.

Avoid coverage for small, frequent losses and extend coverage and limits for those rare but catastrophic exposures. To further save money wisely, ask your insurance agent to suggest loss prevention and loss reduction measures that you can undertake. Some insurers reduce premiums for properties equipped with hurricane shutters, fire sprinkler systems, burglar bars, and dead-bolt locks.

Shop & Contrast

Secure competitive quotes from at least three to four other sources. You might also solicit bids through several insurance websites that are operating, such as www.insurance.com, make sure to check with National Real Estate Insurance Group (recommended by Clint Coons), and Chubb. Of course, don’t jump for the company with the lowest premiums. Through Best’s Guide or other consumer rating reports, verify the company’s financial strength and is willingness to pay claims. Because property and liability insurers are licensed by state governments, check the company’s record of performance with your state department of insurance. (Insurance company financial data and performance records can be obtained at your local library or through your state department of insurance website).
